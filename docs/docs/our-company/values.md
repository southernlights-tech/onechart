# Introduction

These principles are designed to be more than just statements on a wall; they are intended to serve as **actionable decision-making criteria** for every member of the team. For them to be effective, these principles must accurately reflect the real organization and its strategic goals.

The objective is to provide a distinct and highly actionable framework, differentiating them from the typically articulated values found in a company's Mission, Vision, and Values (MVV) statements. The core distinction lies in their purpose: they are integrated as direct decision-making criteria, rather than merely aspirational declarations.

# Rationale for the Order

1. **Integrity**: This is the cornerstone of any successful organization. It builds trust with employees, customers, and stakeholders, which is crucial for long-term success.  
2. **Commitment**: A strong commitment to the company's vision and values ensures that everyone is aligned and motivated to achieve common goals.  
3. **Teamwork**: Collaboration is vital in a startup environment where resources may be limited. Effective teamwork fosters creativity and problem-solving.  
4. **Courage**: Startups often face uncertainty and risk. Courage encourages taking calculated risks and making bold decisions.  
5. **Initiative**: Encouraging employees to take initiative can lead to proactive problem-solving and a culture of ownership.  
6. **Innovation**: As a startup, being innovative is essential for differentiation and growth. This value drives the development of new ideas and solutions.  
7. **Mastery**: While important, competence can be developed over time. It’s essential to have skilled individuals, but the other values can often compensate for gaps in competence in a startup environment.  
8. **Continuous Improvement**: This value is crucial for growth and adaptation but is often a result of the other values being in place. It reflects a mindset that should permeate the organization.

#  Values

## Integrity

### Description

Telling the truth to oneself and others, having a grounded understanding of reality, seeing things as they are (not as one wishes them to be), and fully participating in debriefs to ensure improvements are based on facts. This promotes a culture of transparency and honest self-assessment.

Maintaining transparency in all communications, whether about project status, technical challenges, or incident post-mortems. A commitment to data-driven decision-making and honest self-assessment.

### Example

* Sharing dashboards with real-time metrics for service health and performance.   
* Conducting blameless post-mortems for every incident, focusing on systemic causes rather than individual error, to foster open discussion and learning.

### Integrity in Action

* See reality. Speak truth. Build trust.  
* Decisions grounded in facts, not wishes.  
* Truthful data fuels genuine progress.  
* Transparency builds our strongest foundation.  
* Own the facts, always.  
* Honesty in every interaction.  
* No blame, just lessons and solutions.  
* Ground your understanding. Elevate our standards.  
* Be real. Be reliable. Be integral.  
* Our integrity defines our impact.

## Commitment

### Description

Being present at work, giving one's best, and actively choosing to be there.

Cultivating a deep sense of ownership and dedication to delivering value, both to internal teams and external customers. This goes beyond mere job duties and involves a personal investment in the success of the startup.

### Example

Teams voluntarily staying late to resolve critical production issues, not out of obligation, but because they are personally invested in the service's reliability and user experience.

### Commitment in Action

* Show up, give your best, own the outcome.  
* Your dedication drives our success.  
* Invest in our vision, deliver on our promise.  
* Go the extra mile, make the difference.  
* Committed to excellence, invested in impact.  
* Choose to be here. Choose to excel.  
* Our shared commitment builds our future.  
* Dedication fuels every solution.  
* From idea to impact: all in.  

## Teamwork

### Description

Working collaboratively because a mistake by one can lead to disaster for all. It means not undercutting each other and actively backing each other up in a positive way, explicitly contrasting with internal competition. Mechanisms should be implemented to encourage and reward teamwork.

Fostering seamless collaboration between development, operations, quality assurance, and security teams throughout the software development lifecycle. This involves shared goals and collective responsibility for service health.

### Example

* The "deliberate action" mechanism fostered teamwork by allowing adjacent operators to intervene and correct potential mistakes before they happened, effectively stopping error propagation within the team.  
* Implementing cross-functional teams that share responsibility for a service from design to operations.  
* Shared on-call rotations that include both developers and operations engineers to foster empathy and shared understanding.

### Teamwork in Action

* Collaborate. Elevate. Succeed together.  
* One team, one mission, shared success.  
* Your expertise strengthens our collective.  
* We rise by lifting each other.  
* Back each other up, always.  
* Seamless together. Stronger as one.  
* Collective responsibility, powerful results.  
* No blame, just solutions, together.  
* When we work as one, anything is possible.  
* Shared goals. Unified effort.

## Courage

### Description

Courage: Means choosing "**to do the right thing, even if it may be uncomfortable**," including "**admitting mistakes, even if ugly**". This builds a culture of genuine accountability and learning from failures, rather than hiding them due to fear of punishment. It also means not just doing or saying what subordinates, peers, or superiors want to see or hear.

Fostering an environment where engineers feel safe to challenge assumptions, report bugs or security vulnerabilities without fear of blame, and admit when a project is off track or a new tool isn't working as expected.

### Example

* An engineer publicly admitting a mistake during an incident retrospective and explaining how they will prevent it in the future, fostering a blameless culture.   
* Standing up to a client who demands a feature that would compromise system stability.

### Courage in Action  
* Do the right thing, always.  
* Speak up. Stand strong.  
* Own your mistakes; learn from them.  
* Challenge assumptions, build better solutions.  
* Integrity over comfort.  
* The truth makes us stronger.  
* Fear less, innovate more.  
* Be accountable. Be brave.  
* Honesty fuels progress.  
* **Courage to act, wisdom to learn.**

## Innovation

### Description

Encourages "looking at new ways of doing the same thing" and having the "courage to change, and tolerating failures". This emphasizes practical application and resilience in the face of new approaches.

Fostering a culture of experimentation with new technologies, tools, and processes across development, operations, and security. This involves creating safe-to-fail environments for trying out novel solutions.

### Example

Experimenting with a new ~~serverless~~ architecture for a specific service or adopting a new ~~chaos engineering~~ tool to proactively identify system vulnerabilities.

### Innovation in Action  
* Dare to explore new horizons.  
* **Embrace change, even when it's uncomfortable**.  
* Innovate, iterate, and inspire.  
* Learn from every attempt; success is built on resilience.  
* Challenge the status quo, create the future.  
* Your next great idea starts with a brave step.  
* Experiment fearlessly, grow endlessly.  
* **Turn ideas into impact.**  
* The path to progress is paved with new approaches.  
* Courage to change. Will to innovate.

## Initiative

### Description

Defined as taking action "without direction from above to improve our knowledge... and come up with solutions". This goes beyond typical corporate encouragement of "proactivity" by placing an "obligation on the chain of command not to stifle initiative".

Encouraging engineers to proactively identify and resolve issues, automate repetitive tasks, or propose improvements to processes, even if it falls outside their immediate assigned duties.

### Example

An engineer noticing a recurring issue in a deployment pipeline and proactively designing and implementing an automated fix, or suggesting a new monitoring tool that could prevent future outages.

### Embrace Initiative  
* Act. Don't wait.  
* See a need, fill a need.  
* Lead the way, even when no one is watching.  
* Challenge the status quo. Improve what's next.  
* Your ideas, our progress. Make them happen.  
* Curiosity sparks solutions. Pursue it.  
* Own the problem, deliver the fix.  
* Build it better, then build it again.  
* Go beyond. Innovate within.  
* Initiate. Elevate. Dominate.

## Mastery

### Description

Stresses individual responsibility for learning one's area, basing decisions on "technical reasons, not hope". This is critical for effective decentralized decision-making in a complex environment.  "Control without competence is chaos"

Ensuring every team member has a comprehensive understanding of the entire software delivery pipeline, from code commit to production deployment, including underlying infrastructure, cloud services, and security protocols

### Example

* Regular "tech deep dives" where engineers present and discuss the intricacies of a specific system or technology.  
* Encouraging certifications in cloud platforms (AWS, Azure, GCP) or specific DevOps tools.  
* Encouraging poc/experimetation or specific DevOps tools.

### Mastery in Action  
* Master your craft.  
* Decisions rooted in knowledge, not assumptions.  
* Dig deep, build strong.  
* Own your expertise.  
* Continuous learning, confident decisions.  
* Understand the 'how' and the 'why.'  
* Knowledge empowers control.  
* Seek depth. Deliver excellence.  
* **Become the expert. Shape the solution.**  
* Our competence, our strength.

## Continuous Improvement

### Description

Constantly seeking ways to learn from processes, improve them, and improve oneself. The leadership is obligated to institute mechanisms for this, such as debriefs.

Implementing a "[Kaizen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaizen)" philosophy where small, iterative improvements are constantly made to development, deployment, and operational workflows. This heavily relies on feedback loops and iterative refinement.

### Example

* Shift from passive "briefings" to active "certifications" ensured that every watch stander was intellectually engaged and prepared, leading to increased competence and improved performance during operations.   
* Critiques of incidents, conducted to learn and prevent recurrence, rather than just assign blame.  
* Regularly scheduled retrospectives after every sprint or major release, focusing on process improvements. Implementing A/B testing on deployment strategies to find the most efficient and reliable methods.

### Continuous Improvement in Action  
* Learn. Adapt. Grow.  
* Better every day, in every way.  
* Seek feedback, drive progress.  
* Refine the process, elevate the outcome.  
* Today's lesson fuels tomorrow's success.  
* Innovate through iteration.  
* Kaizen: Small steps, big impact.  
* Challenge what's good, achieve what's great.  
* Embrace critique, empower growth.  
* The pursuit of perfection is a journey, not a destination.
